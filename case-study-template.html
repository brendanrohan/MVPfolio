Case Study

Number Transfer Redesign: Reducing Customer Friction Across Channels

Role

Senior Experience Designer

Company

Verizon

Timeline

12 months

Image Placeholder

Overview

I led the redesign of Verizon's number transfer (porting) experience, transforming a fragmented, opaque process into a transparent, self-service journey across retail, web, app, and IVR channels.

The Challenge

When customers transferred their mobile numbers to Verizon, 33% experienced "fallout"—failures requiring manual intervention by customer service representatives. This created significant operational costs and poor first impressions for new customers.

Key Metrics

  • • 9.4M ports annually
  • • 314K customer service calls per month (4% of all calls to Verizon service)
  • • 75% of transfers initiated in retail stores, then escalated to call centers
  • • The 33% fallout rate required manual resolution with customer service

Root Cause: Customers lacked critical information (account numbers, PINs from their old carrier) at the point of sale. With no visibility into the transfer status or ability to self-correct, they had to troubleshoot on their own—forcing channel-hopping between stores, websites, and customer service.

Research and Discovery

What we did

We conducted user interviews and collaborated with product managers to create a comprehensive service blueprint mapping the end-to-end porting journey across all touchpoints.

What we learned

Channel confusion: Customers bounced between retail, web, and phone with inconsistent messaging at each touchpoint

Information opacity: The transfer process felt like a "black box"—customers had no visibility into status or next steps.

Messaging chaos: We inventoried dozens of error messages with conflicting language and unclear action items

System fragmentation: Legacy backend systems across retail, IVR, and digital channels didn't communicate effectively

Strategic Approach

Rather than rebuild legacy systems, we designed a unified customer experience layer that worked across existing infrastructure—giving customers agency and visibility regardless of where they started.

1

Unified Messaging Framework

Audited and streamlined scores of status and error messages down to four core types with clear, actionable language. Added proactive "device waiting to ship" notifications to set expectations.

2

Retail to Digital Handoff

Created an SMS-based bridge for the 75% of customers who started in stores:

  • • Sent automated text messages to customers who initiated transfers
  • • Linked to a mobile web form for missing information
  • • Provided real-time status tracker for completed transfers

The Impact

15% reduction in fallouts in the year following launch (2022):

  • • Significant reduction in the 314K monthly support calls
  • • Multi-million dollar operational cost savings
  • • Improved first impressions for new Verizon customers

Lessons Learned

Working With Technical Constraints

This project taught me how to drive customer experience improvements within the reality of legacy systems. Rather than waiting for a perfect technical solution, I focused on the experience layer—unified messaging, proactive communication, and smart channel handoffs.

Cross-Functional Orchestration

Success required aligning retail, digital, IVR, and technical teams around a shared vision. I used the service blueprint as a collaboration tool to build consensus and identify integration points across siloed systems.

Incremental Impact

By starting with the highest-volume channel (retail-to-digital) and the clearest pain point (messaging), we could deliver value quickly while building toward a more comprehensive omnichannel solution.
